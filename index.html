What is MUSHT?

Make Underutilized Spuds Have Taste. If 100 % of every potato Earth grows in a year were extruded into one perfectly straight french fry, 0.75 cm × 0.75 cm square, it would run ~3.84 billion miles — past Pluto's average orbit (41 AU), 154,000× around the equator, a line a photon needs 5.7 hours to cross. Only ~8 % of the crop actually becomes fries, so the real fry line is ~0.30 billion miles — the gap is the story.

MUSHT — Make Underutilized Spuds Have Taste

The hero number: miles of french fry if the whole crop went straight to the fryer. Computed on raw-potato volume (ρ ≈ 1.08 g/cm³), 0.75 cm square cut.

The Fun Number

Extrude all 375 Mt of potato into one perfectly straight 0.75 cm fry and it stretches 3.84 billion miles — 41 AU, past Pluto. But only ~29.8 Mt (7.9 %) actually becomes fries, so the real fry line is 0.30 billion miles. Only about 1 potato in 13 ends up a fry.

Run 2 — miles of fry, by cut. Straight rules (~72 %), but the fun lives in the tail: 🌀 curly fry ≈ 12 million miles a year — ~26 round-trips to the Moon — almost all of it Arby's. Waffle (~15 M mi) is Chick-fil-A's signature and the fastest-growing cut. (Straight-line-equivalent miles; curly/waffle aren't literal straight lines.)

Reproduce it yourself: python3 tools/musht.py. The mass-balance gate that keeps the fates summing to 100 %: python3 tools/massbalance.py.

Run 3 — cut by restaurant tier. The chains are the short head; the independent / non-chain tail (~half of restaurant units — diners, chippies, food trucks, street vendors) is the largest, most varied fry-volume tier. Chains anchor the signatures — waffle = Chick-fil-A, curly = Arby's, crinkle = Shake Shack / Culver's, steak fries = Red Robin, hand-cut = Five Guys — the tail fills in everything else.

Run 4 — numbers on the tail. US foodservice = ~422k independent vs ~263k chain units; independents hold ~67 % of sales. Chains over-index on fries (~45 % of fry volume), but the independent tail still carries ~55 %. And the global fries scale checks out top-down: McCain alone makes ~1 in 4 fries worldwide (Lamb Weston #2, Simplot ~3 Mt) — corroborating the ~30 Mt raw / ~15 Mt finished stream.

TPS — Taters Per Second

The rate metric: how fast Earth grows potatoes, counted as whole tubers (avg ~150 g). A deliberate nod to Universe of Finance's TPS — transactions per second.

The Rate Number

Earth produces ~79,000 taters every second — 2.5 trillion a year. At that rate the planet grows potatoes faster than the entire global financial system settles transactions (~73,750/sec): 🥔 > 💸, about 1.07×.

Two views, as asked: a flat annual-average TPS, and a month-by-month TPS — each normalised so the twelve months reconcile exactly to the annual figure. Run 4 adds a both-hemisphere GLOBAL curve (orange): far flatter than the Northern-Hemisphere line (green), because the Southern Hemisphere's seasons offset the North and most of the world's potato eaters have weak seasonality. The holiday spike (a 24 % swing in the NH) shrinks to just ~10 % globally — Thanksgiving is a Northern/Western phenomenon. Reproduce: python3 tools/tps.py.

PPC — Pennies Per Calorie

Where is it cheapest to actually eat a fry? PPC prices the only thing a fry delivers — energy: US cents ÷ calories. It's a ratio, so a medium chain fry and a whole retail bag compare directly.

The Value Number

Home wins by a mile — a raw-potato calorie costs 0.24¢, ~8× cheaper than fine-dining frites (2.00¢). And the big surprise among restaurants: Five Guys is the cheapest fry per calorie (0.54¢) despite feeling pricey — its giant 953-cal portion beats McDonald's, which is 2× dearer per calorie.

US 2025 medium/regular prices (vary widely by location) ÷ published calories. Measures cost-of-energy, not health. Reproduce: python3 tools/ppc.py.

Run 6 — the Fry Index. Price a McDonald's medium (320 cal) around the world, à la the Big Mac Index: a fry calorie costs ~4.7× more in Zurich than in Mumbai (0.41 → 1.93 ¢/cal). And over time — fry-flation — the US McDonald's fry is up ~3.9× since 2000, dearer per calorie than general inflation.

CPI — the Consumed Potato Index

What share of a country's calories comes from potatoes — and from fries specifically? (A pun on the other CPI.) The surprise: the most potato-dependent nations are not the most fry-dependent.

The Diet Number

Rwanda & Belarus get ~11 % of all calories from potatoes — but almost entirely boiled/fresh. The fry nations are the Anglo/Western ones: the UK (2.5 %) and USA (2.0 %) draw the most calories from fried potato despite eating far less potato overall. Two different relationships with one tuber.

By income decile (USA). Potato-calorie share falls with income (4.6 % → 2.4 %) — the cheap staple is leaned on more at the bottom, straight out of PPC. The fry curve is flatter: the rich eat fast food more often, but it's a smaller slice of a larger, more varied diet.

The Value-Add Battery — potato → fry

Richer people eat far fewer potatoes — but not fewer fries. So how much value does frying a potato actually add? Here are four ways to measure it, and they all agree.

The Economics Number

MCE-PF — Marginal Consumption Elasticity of Processing a Fry. The raw potato is a textbook inferior good (income elasticity ≈ −0.4 to −1.2); the fry is roughly normal (≈ 0 to +0.2). So MCE-PF = ε_fry − ε_potato ≈ +0.5 — frying adds ~+0.5 of income-elasticity, turning an inferior good into a normal one. That's why the whole processing industry exists.

ε — raw potato (inferior)−0.4 to −1.2
ε — fry (≈ normal)0 to +0.2
MCE-PF (elasticity added)≈ +0.5
Calorie multiplier (oil)~4×
WTP premium (¢/cal)up to ~8×
Farmer's share of a QSR fry~1.4% (~5¢)

VAM — the value ladder. A medium fry's ~220 g of potato is worth ~5¢ at the farm gate but sells for $3.56 at a QSR — a ~74× multiple, of which only ~8× is retail and the rest is frying, cooking, serving and brand. Fine-dining frites reach ~114×. Every lens says the same thing: the fry is what a potato becomes to survive rising income. Reproduce: python3 tools/vam.py.

The Fry Bill — the induced supply chain

The fry doesn't just add value — it induces a whole supply chain. How many potatoes exist only because we fry, and what does frying the world cost, sector by sector?

The Footprint Number

~18 Mt of potato demand — ~4.8 % of all world production — exists only because we turn potatoes into fries. Absent the fry, ~40 % reverts to fresh boiled potato and ~60 % simply vanishes (the income-elastic demand the fry sustains — see MCE-PF). Everything below is the supply chain built to serve it.
